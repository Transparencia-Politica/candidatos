**Source:** Marzuca, A., Serdült, U., & Welp, Y. (2011). *Questão Pública: First Voting
Advice Application in Latin America.* In Tambouris, Macintosh & de Bruijn (Eds.), *ePart

> run for a Brazilian national election. It documents what was tried, what broke, and gives

> [doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-23333-3_19](https://doi.org/10.1007/978-3-642-23333-3_19) (paywalled).

## 1. What it was

- **Questão Pública (QP)** — `questaopublica.org.br` — the **first VAA in Latin America**.
- Built for the **2010 Brazilian Senate elections** (election day **3 October 2010**), which
  renewed **two-thirds of the 81 Senate seats (54 seats)**.
- Explicitly **both research and political activism** — framed as a transparency/accountability
  tool, deliberately focused on the **Senate** to counter the media's presidential-race focus.

## 2. Who built it

A consortium of NGOs + universities:
- **ABRACCI**, **Avina Foundation**, **Ethos Foundation**, **Agora Institute**,
  **Centre for Research on Direct Democracy (c2d)**, **Transparency International**, **Gov2u**.
- Academic leads: Marzuca, Serdült, Welp (ZDA / Uni Zurich + UCU Uruguay).

This NGO+academic stewardship model is the same credibility pattern as Germany's bpb — an
independent, visibly non-partisan operator. (See `wahl-o-mat-methodology.md`.)

## 3. Design decisions (the three VAA levers)

The paper frames every VAA around **three** decisions:

### a) The questionnaire
- **35 issue statements**, written by specialists, vetted by the NGO network, and **tested
  with different social groups** before launch.
- **Tension noted:** NGOs vs. academics disagreed on some questions — "crucial for some,
  biased for others." A real, documented risk in thesis selection.
- **Response scale:** five points — *absolutely agree / agree / neither / disagree /
  absolutely disagree* — **plus** "not interested" and the option **not to answer**. The paper
  stresses that whether you allow neutral / not-interested / no-answer for users *and*
  candidates materially changes the matching result.

### b) Positioning (where party/candidate answers come from)
Three options exist: (1) non-partisan expert assessment, (2) the parties themselves, (3)
individual candidate responses. **QP used only option 3 — individual candidate answers.** This
is the source of its biggest weakness (see §5: low response → missing data).

### c) Linking preferences & the matching algorithm
- They tested several models (scalar products, matrix models, rank-order) and **chose a
  Euclidean distance model** for producing **stable results**.
- The paper warns: *every* parameter (matching coefficient, % match, distance-vs-directional
  model, perception of a "good match") affects validity.

## 4. Question-quality methodology (the important part)

This section is the academic backbone for *how to choose theses* and reinforces the
Wahl-O-Mat *Trennschärfe* principle:

- The **catalog must cover the political spectrum of all parties/candidates**, or the result
  is biased.
- **Ideal practice (Walgrave et al., "statement selection"):** pre-test each candidate
  statement with a **representative survey**, keeping the ones that produce an **"M-shaped"
  answer distribution** — many answers on each side, few in the middle. That M-shape **is**
  discriminating power: the statement splits people into distinct camps.
- QP **couldn't afford** that survey, so it built the questionnaire from **secondary data and
  literature** — notably **Power & Zucco's ideology estimates of Brazilian legislative parties
  (1990–2005)**.
- Key caution: *"one and the same question can perform very well in one electoral system and
  context but not in another."* Don't blindly copy a German thesis set.

> **Takeaway for 2026:** the M-shaped / pre-test idea maps exactly onto our plan to use **real
> roll-call votes** to *measure* a thesis's discriminating power (behavioral *Trennschärfe*)
> instead of running a costly survey.

## 5. Outcomes & the cautionary tale

- **272 eligible candidates** across 27 states; only **58 candidates in 16 states answered**
  (≈21%). Consulted **~8,000 times**; **6,360** users gave socio-demographic data.
- **Massive missing-data / self-selection problem:**
  - **No correlation** between a state's size and its response rate. São Paulo had 66.7% of
    users but only **33%** of its candidates answered (5 of 15); Rio Grande do Sul had 78%
    candidate response but only 2.3% of users.
  - **Small / new-party candidates answered more** (incentive to gain visibility);
    **major-party incumbents avoided controversial positions** (abortion, gay marriage) —
    "catch-all" candidates have little incentive to commit.
  - Of the 58 respondents, **only 4 won seats** (PMDB, PT, PSDB, DEM) — the tool best covered
    the candidates *least* likely to win.
- **Timing problem:** official candidate list came end of July, election early October. Getting
  answers took weeks of outreach → **launch was delayed → reduced impact**.
- **User profile:** young, male, educated, home-internet — the digital-divide skew (68%
  answered from home; most users 21–40).

### Lessons the authors draw (directly reusable)
1. Contact candidates **early** with a **solid outreach strategy**; expect slow responses.
2. Work **through networks** to create a **snowball** effect across parties.
3. **Don't underestimate mass media** for diffusion.
4. Run the VAA on **several websites with a shared database**, not one site.

> **The #1 lesson for 2026:** relying on *candidate self-reporting* gives you sparse, biased
> coverage (and the strongest candidates opt out). This is the strongest argument for our
> approach: **derive incumbents' positions from their actual votes** (Câmara/Senado open data),
> so coverage doesn't depend on anyone choosing to answer. Self-report only fills gaps for
> non-incumbent challengers.

## 8. How this updates our design

1. **Self-report is a trap** for the candidates who matter most — confirmed empirically (only
   4 of 58 respondents won). → **Behavioral data (votes) first; self-report only to fill gaps.**
2. **Reuse & refresh the 35-statement bank** as a seed *tese* set; drop dated items, re-anchor
   survivors to specific 2026-era bills/votes, and add current issues (e.g. digital regulation,
   environmental enforcement, fiscal framework).
3. **Pre-test for discrimination is essential** — we get it "for free" by measuring how a
   roll-call actually split the houses (behavioral *Trennschärfe*).
4. **Outreach + timing**: the TSE registration deadline (**15 Aug 2026**) is the hard start for
   candidate data; plan ingestion + any candidate-survey outreach around it.
5. **Distribute, don't centralize** — the authors recommend multiple front-ends on a shared
   database; aligns with shipping an API/MCP layer others can embed.
